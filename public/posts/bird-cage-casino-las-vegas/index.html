A Cage Full of Big Dreams: Introducing the Bird Cage Casino

The Modern Look and That Iconic Sign

Approved in late 1957, the Bird Cage was the brainchild of former Los Angeles bakers, Stewart R. Kennard Jr. and Maurice W. Fortney. They envisioned a modern casino featuring seven table games and 102 slot machines, fueled by a $218,000 bankroll. But what truly set the Bird Cage apart was its dazzling exterior. Above the entrance, suspended high above the street, hung a massive, 10-foot birdcage sign. Inside, giant, illuminated red dice tumbled, creating a spectacle that instantly became a downtown landmark. Postcards and photographs of the era almost always included the Bird Cage alongside its established neighbors, solidifying its place in the visual landscape of vintage Las Vegas.

Inside the Compact Casino

Stepping inside, the Bird Cage was quintessential 1950s Vegas: a compact space crammed with rows of slot machines, a small selection of table games, and a bustling bar serving thirsty patrons. It wasn’t flashy in terms of opulence; it was a practical, working-class casino designed to attract locals and tourists alike. While positioned on a prime corner, promising a wave of foot traffic, the competition downtown was brutal.

A Quick Demise, a Lasting Legacy

Sadly, the Bird Cage’s time was short. Despite the eye-catching sign and its strategic location, it struggled to maintain profitability and closed its doors after just 16 months.
