The Forgotten Elwell Hotel & Casino: A Vintage Las Vegas Story

A Family’s Legacy in the Desert Bloom

Before the mega-resorts, before the themed hotels, and even before the Strip truly solidified its dominance, Las Vegas was built on the dreams and hard work of families like the Elwells. The story of the Elwell Hotel and Casino is a quintessential example of early Las Vegas ‚Äì a place where ambition, ingenuity, and a little bit of luck could build something from the desert floor.

In 1946, William Elwell, alongside his sisters Naomi Uptigrove and Doris Brown, began construction on a hotel at the intersection of First and Carson Streets. This location, now just a memory, sits roughly where the Golden Nugget’s parking garage stands today. The land itself held significant history, being property held by the Elwell family who were amongst the early settlers of Las Vegas. After a few years of planning and construction, the Elwell Hotel officially opened its doors in 1951.

A Simple, Solid Start

The Elwell wasn’t flashy. It didn’t boast a towering facade or a dazzling show. What it did offer was a comfortable, reliable place to stay for visitors arriving in the burgeoning city. It catered to a different crowd than the glamorous casinos on Fremont Street ‚Äì a solid, dependable option for those looking for a place to rest their heads after a long day. The initial hotel held a modest number of rooms, reflecting the scale of Las Vegas at that time.

Growth and a Brief Casino Venture

The mid-1950s brought expansion. In 1954, the Elwell Hotel was enlarged to accommodate 117 rooms. This expansion reflected the growing demand for lodging as Las Vegas continued its rapid rise. Adding to its appeal, the Elwell briefly incorporated a casino, solidifying its place in the evolving Las Vegas landscape. William Elwell himself was a prominent figure in the community, serving as both a University of Nevada regent and a Las Vegas city commissioner ‚Äì demonstrating the respect and influence the family had gained.

A Passing of the Torch and a Demolition

Sadly, William Elwell passed away in August 1963, marking the end of an era for the family‚Äôs involvement. Just two years later, in 1965, the Elwell Hotel was sold to the Pioneer Club for a substantial $1.5 million. The hotel was then renamed the Pioneer Hotel and continued its legacy as a popular downtown destination. However, the relentless march of progress in Las Vegas ultimately led to its demise. In 1984, the Golden Nugget purchased the property and, in a move that signaled the changing face of the city, the building was demolished to make way for a new parking garage.

The Legacy of a Quiet Pioneer

Though the Elwell Hotel and Casino is now just a memory etched in vintage photographs and recalled by long-time residents, its story speaks volumes about the foundations of Las Vegas. It represents the early days of family-run businesses, the quiet determination of those who helped build the city, and the ever-present pressure of progress that shaped its destiny. Today, that parking garage at First and Carson stands as a silent testament to the Elwell Hotel ‚Äì a forgotten but important chapter in the vibrant history of Las Vegas.
