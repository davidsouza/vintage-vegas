Remembering the Bonanza Hotel & Casino: A Short, Sweet, & Wild Ride on the Las Vegas Strip

A Western Dream Briefly Blooms on the Strip

Before the dazzling mega-resorts, before the choreographed fountains, and even before some of the iconic properties we know today, the Las Vegas Strip was still evolving. A fascinating piece of that evolution was the Bonanza Hotel and Casino, which opened its doors on July 1, 1967, at 3645 South Las Vegas Boulevard. It was a relatively small operation compared to the titans emerging on the Strip. Boasting only 160 rooms, it paled in comparison to the burgeoning luxury resorts, but it aimed to carve out its own niche. The casino, however, ran into financial trouble remarkably quickly, closing just three months after opening. While the casino shuttered, the hotel continued operating, a testament to the initial investment‚Äôs desire to keep some portion of the property alive.

The Bonanza’s fortunes took a turn when the legendary Kirk Kerkorian, a man who shaped the very face of the Las Vegas Strip, purchased the property at the end of 1968. He quickly flipped it to a group of investors a few months later. This new ownership brought a resurgence; the Bonanza reopened in May 1969, revitalized but still fraught with internal conflict.

A significant power struggle between two top investors over management led to a legal battle, eventually settled, highlighting the volatile nature of Vegas business ventures. Despite the behind-the-scenes drama, the Bonanza carved out a niche, becoming a hotspot for country music fans. Sadly, the Bonanza‚Äôs time was short-lived. Kerkorian, ever the shrewd businessman, filed a foreclosure action in 1970, reclaiming control.
