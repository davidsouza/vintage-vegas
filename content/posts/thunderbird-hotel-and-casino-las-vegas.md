## A Southwestern Oasis on the Strip

The Las Vegas Strip wasn't always the glittering behemoth it is today. Back in 1948, it was a burgeoning stretch of Highway 91, and the Thunderbird Hotel and Casino was a vital piece of that early, pioneering landscape. Opened on September 2nd, 1948, it stood as the fourth resort on the burgeoning Strip, a significant milestone in Las Vegas's evolution from a desert outpost to a gambling and entertainment hub. ## Thunderbird Style: Navajo Themes and Neon

The Thunderbird wasn‚Äôt just about gambling; it was about creating an experience. Hicks and Jones wanted to evoke the spirit of the Southwest. The hotel's decor embraced a Southwestern/Navajo theme, prominently displaying portraits of American Indians and cultivating an atmosphere distinct from the more generic hotel designs of the time. The iconic neon Thunderbird statues that graced the entrance became an instant landmark, visible for miles and a powerful symbol of the developing Strip.  

Initially boasting just 79 rooms, the Thunderbird quickly recognized the appetite for more.  A 78-room expansion was announced in February 1949, followed by further growth, bringing the total room count to 206 by 1950. To further accommodate guests, the adjacent 110-room Algiers Hotel, a sister property with its own distinct Moroccan-inspired theme, was built in 1953.  This pairing created a popular destination for visitors seeking variety.
