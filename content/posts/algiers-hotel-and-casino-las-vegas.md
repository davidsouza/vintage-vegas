## A Night in the Arabian Desert, Just Off the Strip

For those of us who cherish the memory of vintage Las Vegas, the Algiers Hotel and Casino holds a special place. Opened on November 25, 1953, it wasn't just another casino; it was an immersive experience. ## The Magic of a Theme

What truly made the Algiers special was its commitment to the theme. While many hotels experimented with different styles, the Algiers largely resisted modernization, stubbornly clinging to its original aesthetic. Walking through its doors felt like stepping into a different world, far removed from the neon glitz of the main casinos. Think cascading fountains, richly colored fabrics, and an air of mystery â€“ it was a deliberate choice to offer a unique and romantic atmosphere. The 110 rooms, while modest in number, were meticulously decorated to enhance the overall theme. This dedication is why many vintage Vegas fans remember the Algiers so fondly; it was a genuine effort to transport guests.

## Years of Change and Near Misses

The Algiers weathered the changing tides of Las Vegas, remaining largely unchanged through decades of development and consolidation. In the early 1990s, the property faced a significant threat: possible condemnation for a street realignment project. Thankfully, this didn't come to fruition. Later, in 2001, owner Larry Kifer attempted a property swap, a common tactic to leverage land value, but the deal ultimately fell through in 2002. This reflected the increasing pressure from development and the rising cost of maintaining a property in a rapidly evolving landscape.

## A Final Farewell and a New Beginning

Sadly, the Algiers Hotel and Casino closed its doors on August 31, 2004, marking the end of an era. Its demolition in March 2005 was a bittersweet moment for Las Vegas history enthusiasts. The property, once a vibrant symbol of a unique and themed approach to hospitality, was cleared to make way for a new chapter. Today, the site is occupied by the Fontainebleau Las Vegas, a modern mega-resort.
