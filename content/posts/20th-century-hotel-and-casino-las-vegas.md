## A Brief, Bright Spark: The 20th Century Hotel & Casino

The Las Vegas Strip is known for its constant evolution, a relentless churn of new resorts and themed experiences. But sometimes, the most fascinating stories are those of places that burned brightly, but didn't last. The 20th Century Hotel & Casino, a short-lived property on Tropicana Avenue between 1977 and 1979, is a perfect example. Its existence ‚Äì just under two years ‚Äì speaks volumes about the era and the ever-present gamble inherent in the Vegas dream.

They envisioned something capturing the optimistic spirit of the 1920s-1940s, a glamorous throwback to the "classic modern" style of the early 20th century. The revamped 300-room resort boasted a casino, buffet, cocktail lounge, live entertainment, and a sparkling pool ‚Äì the standard amenities for a mid-Strip hotel aiming for a sense of sophisticated fun. 

## Capturing a Fleeting Zeitgeist

The name ‚Äú20th Century‚Äù itself was carefully chosen, evoking a sense of timelessness and nostalgia. It attempted to capitalize on a desire for authenticity in an era where Vegas was increasingly becoming synonymous with glitz and spectacle. While the concept aimed for a nostalgic charm, it faced stiff competition. The late 1970s were seeing the rise of megaresorts like the Mirage and the Luxor ‚Äì sprawling empires that fundamentally altered the Las Vegas landscape. The 20th Century simply couldn't compete on scale, and struggled to find its niche.

## A Rapid Succession of Reinventions

The original venture ultimately failed. In January 1979, Herb Ross, already a prominent figure in downtown Las Vegas with the Golden Goose and Coin Castle casinos, took over and swiftly rebranded the property as the Treasury Hotel & Casino, abruptly shifting to a country-western theme. This highlights a critical factor in Vegas: pivoting quickly was (and remains) often essential for survival.

The story doesn't end there. The property continued its transformation. Gerry Philbin, a former NFL player, briefly owned it and ran it as a hotel only when casino licensing issues arose. It declared bankruptcy, saw the DeLillo family briefly regain ownership and rename it the Pacifica Hotel, then the Polynesian Hotel. 

## A Testament to Change

Today, the building at 115 E Tropicana Ave. operates as the OYO Hotel & Casino. While the 20th Century Hotel & Casino itself is gone, its legacy endures. It serves as a poignant reminder of the Strip's volatility and its willingness to experiment ‚Äì and sometimes fail spectacularly ‚Äì in the pursuit of the next big thing. The rapid rebrandings, the thematic shifts ‚Äì they're all chapters in a larger story of a city constantly reinventing itself, perpetually chasing the ever-elusive dream of tourist dollars. It's a classic vintage Vegas tale: a brief flash of ambition, a whirlwind of change, and ultimately, a faded memory in the city's rich and vibrant history.
