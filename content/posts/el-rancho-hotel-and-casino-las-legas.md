## The Birth of a Strip Legend

Before the glitz and grandeur of modern Las Vegas, before the Bellagio fountains and the High Roller observation wheel, there was [El Rancho](/posts/el-rancho-hotel-and-casino-las-vegas/) Vegas. Strategically located at the north end of Highway 91 (now Las Vegas Boulevard), El Rancho was designed to entice travelers from Los Angeles, a crucial market for early Las Vegas development.

The mastermind behind El Rancho's distinctive design was architect Wayne McAllister. He envisioned a rustic, Old West aesthetic, a conscious effort to evoke a romanticized version of Nevada's history. The resort initially featured 65 charming cottage bungalows, creating a unique and intimate feel â€“ a far cry from the mega-resorts we know today. ## Setting the Stage for a Boom

El Ranchoâ€™s popularity wasnâ€™t just a happy accident. It acted as a catalyst for further development. Seeing El Rancho thrive, other entrepreneurs were eager to stake their claim on Highway 91, transforming it from a dusty road into the bustling Strip we recognize. The very existence of El Rancho paved the way for the Flamingo, the [Sahara](/posts/sahara-hotel-and-casino-las-vegas/), and countless others. It demonstrated the potential of Las Vegas as a tourist destination.

Beyond the rooms and gambling, El Rancho introduced a revolutionary concept to Las Vegas: the all-you-can-eat buffet. This innovative approach to dining proved incredibly popular, quickly becoming a Las Vegas staple and remaining a beloved tradition to this day.  The resort also hosted lavish stage shows, attracting performers and guests alike.

## A Tragic End & Lingering Legacy

Despite its initial success, El Rancho's story wasn't destined for a happy ending. The resort changed ownership multiple times throughout the 1950s. Then, on June 17, 1960, a devastating fire ripped through the main building, effectively bringing El Rancho's operations to a halt. While the charming cottages miraculously survived, the heart of the resort was lost.

Following the fire, the property sat largely unused for decades, becoming a vacant reminder of a bygone era. Various redevelopment plans floated around, but nothing materialized. Eventually, Howard Hughes acquired the land, and it remained dormant until MGM Resorts [International](/posts/international-hotel-las-vegas/) transformed a portion into the Festival Grounds in 2015. Today, the land is owned by Phil Ruffin and operates as Las Vegas Festival Grounds, a stark contrast to the romantic Old West charm of El Rancho.
